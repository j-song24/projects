Flow!

Team: Melissa Shi, Emily McDonald, Juliann Fields, Jesse Song

Summary

Flow! Is a fun yet relaxing multiplayer stretching game with the goal of alleviating chronic pain before sleep.

Background

Our clients at Philips tasked our class to solve the problem of interrupted of different user groups. Our focus was to design a game for different contexts of play. We were responsible for learning about the needs of people with chronic pain and their issues around sleep. Furthermore, we defined the barriers they face and created a game that helped them sleep soundly without disturbance.

User Research and Theory

Since our user group of people with chronic pain was very broad, for our initial design , we narrowed down our target group to people with periods. Many women on their periods wake up periodically throughout the night due to several factors including: fear of staining sheets, hot flashes, excruciating cramps and general anxiety and discomfort.

  • Brainstorm and mapping - The first step we took was brainstorming our stakeholders. We wanted to specify who we making a game for and if there are any subsections of our user group
  • Guerilla research - Another one of our first steps was conducting guerilla research- we created a list of interview questions: When did you start having your period? What was your biggest struggle with having period? Why? What is the worst part of having period? Has your period ever affected your sleep? How? What is your coping method for these sleep problems? How did you start this coping method? To further our knowledge about where women felt pain on their periods, we asked women around the HCI masters studio to mark down areas of the body where they have pain. As indicated by the image, most people marked their abdomen area, some marked the back, chest, head, shoulders, quadriceps and shins.

Transformational Goal

We wanted to create a game with the transformational goal of turning an experience that's uncontrollable and painful (like a period) into one where people are able to have agency, control, and comfort over their own bodies.
